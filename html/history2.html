Mar's core has a radius of around 1300 mils and is made up primarily of iron and nickel. It is surrounded by a silicate mantle which formed the dormant volcanoes we see on the surface now. The most abundant elements in Martian crust are iron, magnesium, aluminum, calcium and potassium.

As many already know, the Solar System was formed from a giant rotating ball of gas and dust known as the pre-solar nebula. This dust and gas combined to form balls called proto-planets. Mars was one of these planets and eventually after some time it became the 4th planet and what we now call home!

In general the temperature on Mars is about -80 degrees Fahrenheit and in winter it can get down to -195 degrees F. Spring is the longest season lasting a total of 194 days. Autumn in the northern is the shortest at 142 days. Finally winter lasts around 154 days and summer around 170.

Mars is famous for its large, planet-wide dust storms. Though, Mars has a thin atmosphere, it can still create strong winds. When these winds pick up the fine, dry particles of dust on Mars, a dust storm can occur. Most dust storms cover an area for a few days and carry small particles of dust at speeds of 33 to 66 miles per hour. However, sometimes the storm can cover most of the planet!/p>

close

The composition of the Martian atmosphere differs greatly from Earth's. Earth's atmosphere is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 1.0% argon, 0.04% carbon dioxide, and small amounts of other gases. It also averages about 1% water vapor. Mars' atmosphere however is 95% carbon dioxide, 3% nitrogen, 1.6% argon, and it has traces of oxygen, carbon monoxide, water, methane, and other gases, along with a lot of dust.

Mars is 35.8 million miles away when it is closest to Earth and 249.1 million miles away at its furthest. As Mars orbits the Sun, it completes one rotation every 24.6 hours. Martian days are called sols – short for "solar day." A year on Mars lasts 669.6 sols, which is the same as 687 Earth days.

close

Mars has two small moons: Phobos and Deimos. Phobos (fear) and Deimos (panic) were named after the horses that pulled the chariot of the Greek war god Ares, the counterpart to the Roman war god Mars.
